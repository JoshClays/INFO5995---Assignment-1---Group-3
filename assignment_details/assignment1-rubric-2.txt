security-relevant vulnerability related
to randomness or cryptographic

closely related)

Fix / Mitigation (2 marks)

2 marks: proposes a concrete,
technically sound fix (e.g., replace
weak randomness with a secure
alternative) and explains why it
works.

1.5 marks: proposes a reasonable fix
addressing the main risk, but with
minor missing technical
detail/justification.

0.5–1 mark: fix is vague, incomplete,
or only partially addresses the real
problem.

0 marks: no fix or mitigation, or
suggestion does not address the
vulnerability.

Recorded Presentation & Q&A
(3 marks)

3 marks: recorded talk (≤ 5 minutes)
clearly covers model, vulnerability,

gives strong evidence-based
grade/contribution justification.

2 marks: recorded talk (≤ 5 minutes)
covers main points with generally
clear delivery; Q&A is adequate but
with minor grade/contribution
justification gaps.

1 mark: recorded talk misses key
elements or is hard to follow; Q&A
shows limited understanding or weak
grade/contribution justification.

0 marks: no presentation submitted,
or recording is unusable.

Modified 11 Mar (grading clarification): The rubric above determines marks. The detailed tasks in the assignment specification ask for more
scaffolding/evidence than is scored directly here, so treat those extra items as supporting materials rather than separate marking buckets. Tutors will
check both the submitted materials and the recorded presentation/tutorial Q&A. For Assignment 1, the main focus is the presentation and in-person
Q&A, because they are the strongest evidence that the group understands and has validated the submitted work. If submitted work cannot be explained
or defended, marks may be reduced under the relevant criteria above.
Modified 11 Mar (scope): Assignment 1 is marked only on vulnerabilities related to randomness and cryptography in the provided APK. You are not
expected to investigate every possible vulnerability class in this app. Spending time on unrelated issues will not increase your Assignment 1 mark; save
broader vulnerability hunting for Assignment 2 Part B.
Modified 11 Mar (AI use): Using AI to write the report, generate PoC material, or support analysis is permitted. However, you are responsible for
validating every claim, code fragment, and result you submit. Technical errors, unsupported claims, broken PoCs, or weak answers in Q&A may reduce
marks under the relevant criteria above.

Modified 11 Mar (required submissions): The required items are report.pdf, ai-log/, pocs/ (videos + README), presentation.mp4, and
activity-log.pdf. Missing any required item incurs a 5-mark penalty from the Assignment 1 total. Supporting scripts/configuration are recommended
but optional.
Timing penalty: presentations must be no longer than 5 minutes. For every additional 10 seconds over the limit, 1 mark will be deducted from the
Assignment 1 mark (out of 15), even if the rubric performance would otherwise be higher.
Updated 27 Feb (presentation fairness): in the 5-minute presentation, each team member must speak for at least 40 seconds. If a student’s speaking
time is t < 40 seconds, that student’s individual Assignment 1 mark becomes ⌊M × t/40⌋, where M is the team Assignment 1 mark (rounded down to
an integer). Example: if M = 14 and t = 39, then ⌊14 × 39/40⌋ = ⌊13.65⌋ = 13.
Tutorial format: each group has up to 10 minutes in total (strict): 5 minutes for presentation playback and up to 5 minutes for Q&A if tutors have
doubts.
Updated 27 Feb (contribution fairness): teams must submit activity-log.pdf with each member’s contribution summary. Tutors may adjust
individual grades if contributions are not roughly equal.
